An app that only works on February 29th.

A scavenger hunt through dim sum parlors with guides for each dish.

A multiplayer game designed for a single dinner party that self-destructs when the evening ends.

These aren't billion-dollar startup ideas. They're something potentially more interesting: software built for creative expression, not because they're going to give their investors 100x. In fact, they don't need investors at all.

Building software is cheaper than it's ever been. You don't need to hire an expensive engineer or dev shop anymore. Enterprising creatives with an idea can now prototype it themselves. Most people talk about the consequences in terms of big companies slashing their hiring budgets, or teams moving faster with fewer people. But I think there's another interesting shift happening — ideas that used to be too expensive, too niche, or too fleeting to ever justify building can finally exist, and sometimes in ways that make money.

## When Things Get Cheaper, More Expression Happens

We've watched this play out before. Nearly every now-expressive medium — think food, clothing, metalwork, photography — began as labor-intensive, expensive, and therefore quite practical.

Clothing made to protect against the elements; food prepared to be nourishing enough to labor; photographs used to document and pursue science. However, as each medium became cheaper and more accessible, they exploded into new forms of self-expression.

Clothing was once entirely about staying warm and dry. Fashion now occupies the entire spectrum from function to [artistic expression](https://nyfw.com/). Food followed the same path. Imagine telling a caveman about [Alchemist](https://alchemist.dk/the-alchemist-experience/).

This doesn't mean that functional uses, or scaled commercial products go away. It just means that artistic expression and smaller commercial ideas are both viable as costs of production go down.

Software is primed for the same trajectory.

## Software's Creative Moment

Software is hitting an inflection point with the advance of AI and the ubiquitousness of educational resources.

You can already see examples of what is to come.

- Replit has a gallery of apps for both [Personal](https://replit.com/gallery/life) and [Work](https://replit.com/gallery/work) that were ostensibly built by regular folks who were not professional developers.
- My friend [Harish](https://www.harishvenkatesan.com/), an OG [5x5](http://5x5.studio) contributor, built a game called [Bot or Not](https://botornot.is/) without any game development experience. Right now, there is no financial layer, but it's easy to imagine one. Betting on the outcome of the game seems natural and like a perfectly reasonable form of betting. Imagine contributing 50c like an old arcade, which contributes to a winner's pool that pays out to the highest scorer for the day. Or — just bet as much as you want on the outcome of one round. Regardless, a financial layer that works well with the game is likely just a day's work away.

- MSCHF is releasing interesting hybrid art and commercial experiments, sometimes in the physical world, always connected in some ways to the [digital world](https://ourcowangus.com/). They're already living this "artisanal software" ethos.

When creation becomes cheap and widespread, expression inevitably follows. Get ready for artisanal software, boutique apps, and bespoke digital experiences that say as much about the user as it does the app.

## New Business Models For Artisanal Software

The lower cost of building software should also mean cheaper software.[1](#footnote-1) It will almost certainly mean that more people can build meaningful things without raising outside capital. That might also mean compressed returns - whether due to more competition, or commodification of both labor and outputs.

The good thing is, even in these periods of compressed returns, quality will be valued at a premium, and it will also be possible to take more shots on goal and output more software in the same amount of time, even if each project nets less money. Of course, getting attention and converting it into distribution will continue to be a challenge, but that's orthogonal to the point of this thought.

I'm excited about the business models that might make sense for these smaller ideas, and in some cases may deliver unexpected returns.

A few idea starters:

- Limited edition software that only 100 people can use at a time. Once you're finished, you can rent it out, sell it (perhaps even as an auction), or hold on and see if it's more valuable later. Software as collector's item.

- Software as industrial design object. Design firms like [teenage engineering](https://teenage.engineering/) or [CW&T](https://cwandt.com/) accrue followings that anticipate their every drop
  - Software makers with good taste can build a following and drop small experiences, games, digital art with anticipation.
  - I would expect to see some folks experiment with subscriptions for software in the vein of Substack and Patreon, while others go with a pay-per-use or drop model.

- Open-source + customizable, paid UIs are like open schematics + commercial hardware. When software is built either as open-source and/or as a protocol rather than a single implementation, it opens the door for commercial folks to build UIs that are the portal for most every-day users.
  - Most users won't want to deploy the open-source code themselves. In fact, they may even prefer to use a UI made by a favorite artist-developer.
  - We see this with hardware, like modular synths. The schematics are usually open, but the average musician would rather buy an implementation from their favorite synth companies, who might choose a different metal or plastic, paint or etching, knobs or circuit boards than the next synth company.

Ultimately, I'm excited to see individual software makers or small software design firms being recognized more like artists or craftspeople. They'll generate ideas constantly, build the ones that speak to them, and gradually cultivate audiences who appreciate their particular aesthetic and way of thinking about problems.

The future of software isn't just about making things faster[2](#footnote-2), cheaper and more scalable[3](#footnote-3)—it's about making things that matter to someone, even if that someone is a very specific audience of twelve people who happen to love exactly what you've built.

<a name="footnote-1"></a>[1] For fellow econ nerds out there — we saw a similar argument when the physical economy became more digital, and the marginal cost of producing physical goods went to nearly 0 to produce digital goods. That prediction was largely right; software tends to be cheaper than hardware, even as we've collectively figured out how to monetize 0 marginal cost goods much more precisely than a couple decades ago.

<a name="footnote-2"></a>[2] It will, however, require software artists to move quickly. If more people build software and could potentially build software for themselves essentially for free, it will be harder to charge much for labor or specific pieces of software. Volume, or being so good that you can charge a premium, are the two most likely outcomes. So - make a lot of stuff, make it meaningful, and you'll probably have a chance at making some money.

<a name="footnote-3"></a>[3] Software is unique in its ability to scale quickly. That kind of scale is seductive, and has enabled massive returns for the builders who embrace it. That won't go away - but with so much competition incoming, the competition for scale will be even more fierce than it has been to-date. The big difference now is that with lower cost of creation, those who choose to build software don't have to scale. And with a smaller group of users in focus, they can draw inspiration from other businesses who make a living without massive scale.
