A friend asked me for book recommendations recently, and I realized I've been giving the same recommendations over and over, in some cases for years now. These four books have lodged themselves in my brain and shifted how I see something fundamental: how we understand intelligence, how we process loss, how we relate to other species, how we build food systems.

The list covers a lot of ground: A 1970s thought experiment about robot psychology. A formally experimental novel about Abraham Lincoln's dead son. A slim meditation on symbiosis with nature. A chef's manifesto about American cuisine. They don't obviously belong together on first glance, but they're somehow connected. Tell me if you notice the threads.

- [Vehicles](https://www.amazon.com/experiments-synthetic-psychology-Valentino-Braitenberg/dp/0262022087/ref=sr_1_1?crid=2TRZUVO7Q3DDJ&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.9SIPuc92cHd_8cCiA4n0F45Fof79YK20RC43XtyqQn8.aJzqkBvRdXvF2U6GTyRfOl6nIRHoX0YxmZRjLdinTIs&dib_tag=se&keywords=vehicles+braitenburg&qid=1768066533&sprefix=vehicles+brai%2Caps%2C119&sr=8-1) by Valentino Braitenberg

![Vehicles book cover](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zsXR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F966947df-5629-424d-8aca-b1690d97f925_326x500.jpeg)

Vehicles is a thought experiment that starts with a simple premise — can we understand the behavior of machines that we make. In the first chapter, the machine is just a sensor that moves toward a light source. It's very easy to understand. Each chapter adds one more sensor, each simple on its own, but after just a handful of sensors are all simultaneously operating, the machine begins to show behaviors that look like emotion, or sentience, or even humor.

- Written in the 70s— pre-PC, pre-AI, pre-Internet— and yet more relevant than ever as we again grapple with the implications of machine intelligence.
- This book was initially introduced to me via Dan Shiffman of The Coding Train and NYU ITP, and is made interactive in a number of his [Nature of Code](https://natureofcode.com/) examples that may help you internalize the concepts through doing.

- [Lincoln in the Bardo](https://www.amazon.com/Lincoln-in-Bardo-George-Saunders-audiobook/dp/B01N1NU4K2/ref=sr_1_1?crid=RT7BKVCTFJB7&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.VptNOG1kHlaR0y5weKCsKzz27ZvLZLEVIMJaxN-Awhlm6sRiCNtF8-mv_4eS7SEmuc1BPPd03CkOQbheskK6ndg_astit6D1Zr4sPJUB2WjmdnJa6C3GBcOMjQAZEJ4SRM9qVD2IifVJRykJF-LJ-jwdyRs8HsBqSk4XItn3gjSr9kB__4-LydUnYIPXfgSk6uYJEnkgUck8wk880Khso3AMBJhMCYvPx3oj0kFhksc.5r2gUXt8e5lLcLX4CWe-8ZZcwtZlDckOb89RteBFkMs&dib_tag=se&keywords=lincoln+in+the+bardo&qid=1768066467&sprefix=lincoln+in%2Caps%2C154&sr=8-1) by George Saunders

Follows Abraham Lincoln's son's battle with death and Abraham Lincoln's emotional struggle in letting go, but through whimsical characters inhabiting Lincoln's life in America and the Bardo, the metaphysical space between life and death.

- While some people gravitate towards novels for their plot, I love novels that bend the physics of language-on-a-page. This book is for people like me.
- The narrative structure moves between screenplay to reading like primary source clips from a historical archive (some of which are true archival material, and some of which are fictional, but never revealed to the reader)
- The structure can be disorienting at first for plot-enjoyers, but that's part of what makes it remarkable. Once you grok the structure, the story unfolds with a humor, profundity, and gripping sadness that is singular.

- [Democracy of Species](https://www.amazon.com/The-Democracy-of-Species/dp/0141997044/ref=sr_1_1?crid=3O57CQM24VSIQ&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.ZHhptwRXm0nrmOgBsEgJYsJkcfOMNCpmSCi_V6XlG_tDqlEy8Mi8g6lv5vNdNBwpe8_OrVIDW_MwkILnM9hPid7VDOkNjFlN6dbFEy1MtEDJrGG6siE95stVhGou2Ky4XvBbKs11WASH36poZwn2HI2u7RKgAR21veYYsaanrZ-voxyYnKFu4aj3r4SyJkcEF_2r2mA6FoH2q-RPt0It-EPj0OhsWjFeIVR_DcgBkpk.mFjBG34V01SWHgNf3ffkq8VtRx-LBXaNBw_gEZS7m08&dib_tag=se&keywords=democracy+of+species&qid=1768066489&s=audible&sprefix=democracy+of+species%2Caudible%2C124&sr=1-1) by Robin Wall Kimmerer

A slim book under 100 pages that has burrowed into my mind and feels like a kind of sacred text. In short, the 3 essays in this book show us how to live symbiotically with the world around us.

- The essays themselves are beautifully idiosyncratic: how thinking of nature as verbs instead of nouns rewires our brains, how leaving behind harvest can yield greater abundance in future seasons. They don't come across as new agey voodoo; instead they feel like a reminder of a way of being that fell out of favor in modernity but perhaps for no good reason.

- [The Third Plate](https://www.amazon.com/The-Third-Plate-Dan-Barber-audiobook/dp/B00JZY7B4A/ref=sr_1_1?crid=35GG210ABBBBF&dib=eyJ2IjoiMSJ9.ULybqCG5dD6psEz_DgShkNClNAOrD5tBtLWQFrpuJuE.Ybqu9pW8M_V47vx9-SkhTRkneYjQcd-72FxgcM8blEA&dib_tag=se&keywords=the+third+plate&qid=1768066507&s=audible&sprefix=the+third+plat%2Caudible%2C137&sr=1-1) by Dan Barber 

![The Third Plate book cover](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!69_L!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa5a1227b-f707-4675-a6a1-38364b29277b_329x500.jpeg)

Dan Barber is the chef/founder of Blue Hill at Stone Barns — one of the tastiest and most influential restaurants in America. This is his book on the future of food and what American cuisine could/should be.

- Barber advocates for chefs employing an intimate regional knowledge of the food that grows, grew, and could grow to define an American cuisine led by the land itself.
- He also analyzes the relationship between chef, diner, home cook, and farm system in an astute, aspirational manner that places responsibility in the hands of the chef as a tastemaker and educator.
- This book will change the way you look at the food system in America without being heavy handed or moralizing; instead it's just a series of fascinating experiments and vignettes that take you around the world to learn about things like breeding wheat for flavor instead of pest-resistance, feeding hens bell peppers and noticing their yolks change color and flavor, producing foie gras without force-feeding, and more.
